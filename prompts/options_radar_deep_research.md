# Mandatory Broad-Universe Discovery Engine

## Execution Requirement

Do not begin detailed underwriting from a hand-selected list of familiar securities.

Do not construct the candidate universe primarily from upcoming earnings calendars, mega-cap companies, securities dominating financial news, broad index ETFs, the largest companies in a popular theme, tickers repeatedly mentioned in analyst research, or securities selected merely because their options are highly liquid.

A curated list of obvious names is not an adequate opportunity screen. Research must first perform a broad, reproducible candidate-discovery process designed to uncover underfollowed changes, emerging behavioral signals, unrecognized fundamental inflections, special situations, forced flows, corporate actions, forensic shorts, credit-equity disagreements, option-surface dislocations, delayed estimate revisions, mispriced catalyst probabilities, sufficiently optionable small- and mid-caps, and securities mentioned by differentiated primary sources before broad media coverage.

Only after this discovery process may candidates advance to detailed options underwriting.

---

## Discovery Mode

Use exactly one of the following modes and state which mode was used.

### Mode A — Systematic Market Screen

Use this mode when a database, market screener, or sufficiently comprehensive market-data source is available.

Begin with the broadest reproducible universe available, preferably all US-listed optionable common stocks, optionable US-listed ADRs, REITs, liquid sector and thematic ETFs, and liquid index products. Do not limit the starting universe to the Russell 1000 or S&P 500. Where data permits, include Russell 3000 constituents; S&P 500, 400, and 600 constituents; other optionable securities meeting liquidity requirements; newly optionable securities; recent IPOs after lockup and liquidity review; and recent spinoffs and post-reorganization securities.

Report the starting universe, data provider, observation date, number of securities at the start, every screening rule, the number surviving each rule, the final discovery pool, and coverage gaps. Do not claim a systematic screen unless it can be reproduced.

### Mode B — Source-Led Open-Web Discovery

Use this mode when comprehensive screening data is unavailable. Do not silently replace a systematic screen with a small hand-picked watchlist.

Construct a broad candidate pool using at least ten independent source families and at least sixty named securities before deep underwriting. For each source family, report the source or database searched; query, filter, feed, or page used; relevant date range; number of named candidates found; whether the source is primary, derivative, or social; and important coverage limitations.

Label the final report `SOURCE-LED BROAD DISCOVERY UNIVERSE`. Do not label it an exhaustive market screen.

### Prohibited Discovery Method

“I selected several liquid names with upcoming catalysts” is insufficient. A universe consisting mainly of familiar mega caps, banks, semiconductor leaders, broad ETFs, or prominent geopolitical exposures fails unless those names independently rank highly after the broad screen.

---

## Master Security Universe

Search across these market-cap groups:

- Emerging: approximately $500 million to $2 billion
- Small cap: approximately $2 billion to $5 billion
- Mid cap: approximately $5 billion to $20 billion
- Large cap: approximately $20 billion to $100 billion
- Mega cap: above approximately $100 billion

Market-cap boundaries may be adjusted when justified.

Discovery targets, not final recommendation quotas:

- At least 40% of the raw equity pool should be below $20 billion when sufficiently optionable names exist.
- At least 20% should be below $5 billion when sufficiently optionable names exist.
- No more than 25% of the initial pool should be mega-cap technology, large banks, and broad-market ETFs combined unless the screen justifies more.
- Include at least six sectors and multiple investment horizons.

Never advance an inferior trade merely to satisfy a market-cap, sector, or strategy quota.

---

## Initial Liquidity and Optionability Filters

Do not demand perfect institutional liquidity during discovery because that systematically eliminates potentially asymmetric opportunities. Classify candidates into two tiers.

### Tier 1 — Actionable Optionability

Prefer approximately: 20-day average underlying dollar volume of at least $25 million; multiple usable expirations; strikes around the relevant price; meaningful open interest across the target expiration; realistic limit-order entry and exit; no obvious adjusted-contract complication; and no dependence on one extremely illiquid strike.

Tier 1 candidates may advance toward actionable structures.

### Tier 2 — Emerging Optionability

May include lower but meaningful underlying liquidity, recently listed chains, thin current volume with improving open interest, spreads that may become acceptable, or a high-quality thesis whose ideal entry is conditional on better liquidity.

Tier 2 candidates may advance only as:

`RESEARCH THESIS ONLY — LIVE CHAIN AND LIQUIDITY REQUIRED`

Specify the exact liquidity improvement required before execution. Do not eliminate a differentiated thesis merely because options are not yet execution grade. Eliminate securities where realistic entry and exit are structurally impossible.

## Mandatory Discovery Lanes

Search every lane independently before combining and ranking candidates. Preserve every discovery source and rationale when a security appears in multiple lanes.

### Lane 1 — Fundamental Estimate Inflections

Search for operating evidence that may change before consensus estimates: revenue, bookings, backlog, gross margin, free cash flow, retention, customer-acquisition cost, pricing, new-product monetization, customer concentration, early estimate revisions, analyst disagreement, or guidance stale relative to current evidence. Also search for demand deterioration, inventory accumulation, receivables divergence, pricing pressure, customer losses, margin deterioration, working-capital stress, and repeated guidance reductions. Prioritize evidence before formal analyst revisions.

### Lane 2 — Behavioral and Product-Adoption Signals

Search app rankings and review velocity, product release notes, retail rankings, search trends, web traffic, GitHub activity, package downloads, developer forums, cloud marketplaces, partner directories, customer communities, pricing changes, job postings, creator activity, customer complaints, reseller inventory, and distributor commentary. Require a plausible link to a financially material KPI.

### Lane 3 — Filing and Corporate-Disclosure Signals

Systematically search recent 8-K, 10-Q, 10-K, Form 4, Form 144, 13D, 13G, S-3, S-3ASR, 424B, ATM, debt and covenant amendments, convertibles, warrant exercises, auditor changes, executive departures, and customer or contract disclosures. Look for changes not yet broadly covered.

### Lane 4 — Special Situations and Forced Flows

Search spinoffs, tender and rights offers, merger votes or termination risk, regulatory decisions, index changes, ETF rebalances, lockup expirations, post-bankruptcy equities, separations, accelerated buybacks, capital returns, forced selling, mandate-driven buying, tax-loss selling, and float changes. Identify who is forced to transact and the timeline.

### Lane 5 — Insider and Capital-Allocation Signals

Search clustered or unusually large open-market insider purchases, founder or director purchases, buyback acceleration, debt repurchases, tender activity, insider selling inconsistent with commentary, compensation changes, and policy changes. Distinguish open-market purchases from grants, exercises, and tax transactions.

### Lane 6 — Forensic and Accounting Deterioration

Search receivables or inventory outgrowing revenue, weak operating cash flow, capitalized operating costs, supplier financing, channel stuffing, related parties, aggressive adjusted metrics, auditor turnover, finance-executive departures, restatement risk, covenant pressure, going-concern language, repeated dilution, customer concentration, accounting-policy changes, and risk-factor changes. Generate short candidates and false-positive recovery candidates.

### Lane 7 — Short Interest, Borrow, and Reflexivity

Search high short interest with a real catalyst, rapidly changing borrow cost, declining lendable supply, convertible hedging, warrant or ATM overhang, price-dependent financing, rallies that improve survival, and declines that impair financing or customer confidence. Never advance solely because short interest is high.

### Lane 8 — Credit-Equity and Cross-Asset Disagreement

Search disagreement between equity and bonds, credit spreads, convertibles, commodity inputs, suppliers, customers, valuation, prediction-market probabilities, and industry operating data. Examples include equity optimism while credit deteriorates, credit stabilization before equity recognition, supplier orders improving before customer estimates, or input prices changing faster than margin expectations.

### Lane 9 — Capital-Cycle and Industry Supply Signals

Search capacity additions and closures, utilization, lead times, industry capex, project cancellations, competitor credit withdrawal or bankruptcies, shortages, liquidation, regulatory barriers, and permitting bottlenecks. Do not analyze demand without supply response.

### Lane 10 — Government, Regulatory, and Procurement Signals

Search USAspending, SAM.gov, Federal Register, FDA, ClinicalTrials.gov, CMS reimbursement, FCC, FERC, ISO/RTO, EIA, USPTO, court dockets, state procurement and permits, WARN notices, import/export records, and port or transportation data. Look for awards and modifications, reimbursement changes, approvals, trial progression, delays, patent assignments, permitting, government demand, and legally mandated spending.

### Lane 11 — Option-Surface Anomalies

Screen for IV unusually low versus forecast realized volatility, IV unusually high after uncertainty resolves, skew anomalies, term-structure inversion, excessive or insufficient event premium, adjacent-expiration disagreement, abnormal wings, index-component volatility disagreement, post-event IV dislocation, and large option-price moves without equivalent underlying moves. Do not rely solely on unusual volume.

### Lane 12 — Price and Information Divergence

Search where evidence changed without a stock reaction, stock moved without fundamental evidence, peers reacted but the target did not, reaction was smaller than economic exposure implies, estimates changed but options did not, options changed but stock and fundamentals did not, credit moved first, or product/social evidence accelerated while estimates did not. This lane targets delayed recognition.

## Source-Led Candidate Requirements

When a comprehensive screener is unavailable, search at least ten of these source families: SEC filings; investor relations; earnings and competitor transcripts; supplier/customer disclosures; government procurement; regulatory databases; insider filings; short-interest and borrow data; bonds and credit; index and ETF notices; clinical databases; energy and utility databases; developer activity; app and web traffic; job postings; pricing and documentation changes; retail and distributor evidence; courts and legal filings; social platforms used only for discovery; options volatility, skew, and term structure; trade journals; state/local permits and procurement; and patent/trademark records.

Do not count multiple news articles derived from the same filing, release, or social post as independent sources.

---

## Raw Candidate Pool Requirements

### Systematic Mode Target

Screen at least 500 optionable underlyings where adequate data exists. Report the actual number and preserve filters and survivor counts.

### Source-Led Mode Target

Identify at least sixty named raw candidates, use at least ten independent source families, include at least six sectors and all relevant market-cap groups, include bullish, bearish, volatility, and special-situation candidates, and cover multiple time horizons.

Do not write detailed dossiers for every raw candidate; breadth comes before concentration. If sixty candidates cannot be generated, state which sources were unavailable, which lanes were incomplete, how many were found, and why coverage is limited. Do not silently fall back to seventeen familiar tickers.

---

## Candidate Provenance Ledger

For every raw candidate record: ticker; company or product; sector; approximate market-cap group; discovery lane; exact surfacing source; earliest signal date; one-sentence signal; expected financial, volatility, or flow transmission; next catalyst; stock move since earliest signal; whether estimates reacted; whether mainstream coverage began; initial optionability tier; first rejection risk; and current disposition.

Allowed dispositions: `ADVANCE`, `SECONDARY REVIEW`, `LIQUIDITY WATCH`, `ALREADY PRICED`, `WEAK TRANSMISSION`, `SOURCE CONTAMINATION`, `NO CATALYST`, and `REJECT`.

Include at least the top thirty raw candidates in the final report or appendix.

## Discovery Edge Score

Before the full 100-point opportunity score, assign every serious candidate a Discovery Edge Score out of 20:

- Signal Novelty — 5: 5 for recent underreported primary evidence predating price/estimate changes; 3 partly recognized; 1 widely discussed; 0 selected only for prominence.
- Causal Specificity — 5: 5 for clear measurable material-KPI transmission; 3 plausible but unquantified; 1 weak thematic exposure; 0 no defensible transmission.
- Consensus Lag — 5: 5 for little/no consensus reaction; 3 early revisions; 1 broad recognition; 0 consensus thesis.
- Price and Option Lag — 5: 5 for little recognition; 3 partly priced; 1 large price or IV move; 0 fully priced.

Normally require at least 12 before detailed underwriting. Explicitly justify exceptions. A strong company, famous catalyst, or large possible move is not sufficient.

---

## Wall Street Saturation Filter

For every detailed-underwriting candidate, determine analyst-revision count and recency; presence in consensus commentary; major-media emphasis; social saturation; management promotion; stock and IV move since the first signal; whether the stock moved more than one expected move; whether valuation assumes success; and whether the catalyst is already the primary ownership reason.

Penalize candidates surfaced only by near-term earnings, repeating management’s headline narrative, extreme discussion, already-reflected estimates/valuation, options pricing an unusually favorable distribution, or a stock move that preceded discovery.

Well-covered mega caps may qualify only when the edge is in event magnitude, timing, skew, term structure, correlation, positioning, or a specific structure—not merely a familiar directional opinion.

---

## Advancement Funnel

1. Raw Discovery Pool: preferably 500+ screened underlyings in systematic mode or 60+ named candidates in source-led mode.
2. Initial Triage: reduce to about 20–30 using Discovery Edge Score, causal transmission, catalyst, optionability, evidence quality/freshness, saturation, and first rejection risk.
3. Secondary Underwriting: advance about 10–15 for evidence timelines, expectations, reference classes, counterfactuals, financial transmission, catalyst timing, and preliminary option-surface review.
4. Full Dossiers: advance about 3–8 for research distribution, exact structure comparison, payoff analysis, stress testing, sizing, and entry/exit conditions.

These are target ranges, not recommendation quotas. Do not advance weak candidates to meet a number.

---

## Diversity of Finalists

Unless no qualifying candidates exist, finalists must not all come from one sector, be earnings trades, be mega caps, be broad ETFs, or express the same factor. Include at least two qualifying non-mega-caps in detailed underwriting, at least one credible medium/long-duration candidate, and bearish and volatility opportunities in discovery even if final ranking is bullish. Quality still outranks quotas.

---

## Source-to-Edge Search Logic

For every source, ask:

1. What changed?
2. When was it first observable?
3. Which public company has economically material exposure?
4. Is exposure direct or merely thematic?
5. Which KPI should change?
6. In which reporting period?
7. Have analysts revised?
8. Has the stock moved?
9. Has IV or skew repriced?
10. What catalyst makes the information visible?
11. Why might others have missed it?
12. Is the edge large enough after premium and execution costs?

Do not advance evidence that cannot map to a measurable financial, volatility, or forced-flow outcome.

## Rejected Mainstream Candidates

Include a separate table of at least ten familiar or widely discussed securities considered but not advanced. Possible reasons: no differentiated edge; earnings already priced; consensus thesis; evidence arrived after the move; IV too expensive; mega-cap factor dominance; a better supplier/customer/smaller peer; efficient option surface; obvious catalyst without differentiated probability; attractive stock but unattractive options; or news prominence mistaken for opportunity.

## Anti-Failure Rules for Candidate Discovery

- Do not start from a short familiar watchlist or the next seven days of earnings.
- Do not use famous tickers as a substitute for broad discovery.
- Do not treat liquidity as investment edge or a known catalyst as mispricing.
- Do not claim broad screening without reproducible counts or exhaustive coverage from open-web work.
- Do not silently convert an unavailable systematic screen into a small curated universe.
- Do not select a ticker because reliable information is easy to find.
- Do not overconcentrate in large banks, mega-cap technology, major semiconductors, or broad ETFs.
- Do not eliminate all smaller companies before judging their signals.
- Do not advance illiquid options as actionable trades, but do not ignore a strong underlying thesis solely because the chain is not yet execution grade.
- Do not treat a large possible stock move as positive option expected value.
- Do not present earnings names as novel without a differentiated distribution.
- Do not count repeated reports of one primary event as independent confirmation.
- Do not let mainstream media define the universe.
- Do not claim the best opportunities were found when required lanes/source families were not searched.
- Do not replace the requested opportunity search with a methodology discussion.

The goal is not to find the most recognizable securities. The goal is to discover economically material changes observable before broad recognition, not fully reflected in estimates, stock, or options, paired with an identifiable catalyst, and expressible through a realistic defined-risk trade.
